**Real Map Implementation of DFS**

Ever wondered how algorithms navigate complex spaces? Our latest visualization brings Depth-First Search (DFS) to life on the charming streets of Paris's Marais district! 🇫🇷 DFS is a graph traversal algorithm that explores as far as possible along each branch before backtracking. It prioritizes exploring one neighbor completely before considering others. Internally, DFS typically utilizes a **stack** (either explicit or implicitly through recursion) to manage the order of nodes to visit. When a node is visited, its unvisited neighbors are pushed onto the stack. The algorithm then pops the top node from the stack and repeats the process. This "deep dive" strategy can be incredibly efficient for finding a path, though it might not always find the *shortest* path in unweighted graphs, and can sometimes get "lost" down long branches.

This isn't just an abstract grid; it's a **Real Map Implementation** of graph theory! We've transformed the historical Marais into a graph where intersections represent **nodes** and the intricate network of streets are the **edges**. In our visualization, the vibrant red and green lines trace the **visited edges** as DFS systematically explores the urban labyrinth. The striking yellow line highlights the **computed path** found by the algorithm. Unlike explicit grid implementations where edge traversities are uniform, real-world road networks introduce complexities like one-way streets, varying street lengths, and traffic conditions (though our simplified model assumes traversable edges). This difference significantly impacts the practical performance and potential applications of DFS.
