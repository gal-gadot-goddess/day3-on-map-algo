**Real Map Implementation of DFS**

Ever wondered how algorithms navigate complex networks? In this visualization, we explore Depth-First Search (DFS) in action! DFS is a graph traversal algorithm that explores as far as possible along each branch before backtracking. When encountering a node with multiple unvisited neighbors, DFS arbitrarily chooses one and dives deep. This "go deep first" approach is typically implemented using a **stack** (either explicitly or implicitly via recursion). Unlike algorithms like Breadth-First Search (BFS) which uses a **queue** for level-by-level exploration, DFS prioritizes depth, meaning it will explore a long, winding path before considering alternative routes from an earlier junction. This can lead to a more memory-efficient exploration in certain scenarios but, like a traveler on a winding road, it can sometimes get "lost" and explore suboptimal paths before finding the shortest or desired destination.

This isn't just an abstract grid; we're visualizing DFS on a real-world map of Barcelona's Eixample district! We've transformed the city's intricate network into a graph where intersections are our **nodes** and the streets connecting them are our **edges**. The red and green lines you see highlight the "visited edges" as DFS explores the graph, systematically marking its path. The vibrant yellow line represents the computed path discovered by the algorithm. While an explicit grid might have consistent branching factors, real-world road networks like Barcelona's present a far more complex scenario. Factors like one-way streets, varying street lengths, and irregular intersection geometries significantly impact the performance and complexity of DFS, often requiring adaptations for efficient real-world application beyond simple maze-solving.
