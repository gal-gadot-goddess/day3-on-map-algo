## Real Map Implementation of DFS 🗺️

Ever wondered how algorithms navigate complex environments? Today, we're visualizing **Depth-First Search (DFS)** on a real-world map of San Francisco's Market Street! 🌉

DFS, at its core, is an exploration strategy. Imagine a hiker venturing into a dense forest. They pick a path and go as deep as possible until they hit a dead end or a landmark. Only then do they **backtrack** to the last junction and try another unexplored path. In terms of data structures, DFS typically employs a **stack** (either implicitly through recursion or explicitly with a `Stack` data structure) to keep track of the paths it's exploring. When it encounters a node, it recursively calls itself on the *first unvisited neighbor*. This "first neighbor" prioritization is key – it dictates the direction of the deep dive. Unlike Breadth-First Search (BFS) which uses a queue to explore level by level, DFS is all about plunging ahead! This can lead it to find a solution quickly if the solution path is deep, but it might also wander down many long, unproductive paths before finding its target.

Our San Francisco visualization brings this abstract concept to life! We've transformed the city's street network into a **graph**. Each **intersection** is a **node**, and the **streets** connecting them are the **edges**. The red and green lines you see dynamically tracing the map represent **"visited edges."** The red indicates exploration into a path, and the green signifies backtracking after hitting a dead end or completing a branch. The bright yellow line, on the other hand, is the **computed path** – the optimal route found by our DFS algorithm between two points. It's fascinating to see how DFS tackles the inherent complexity of a real-world road network compared to a simple grid. On explicit grids, the number of neighbors is often fixed (e.g., 4 or 8). However, real roads can have varying degrees of connectivity, with some intersections leading to many streets. This can make DFS's tendency to get "lost" more pronounced, but also potentially faster if the solution path is well-connected and deep within the network. The time complexity of DFS on a graph is generally O(V + E), where V is the number of vertices (intersections) and E is the number of edges (streets).
