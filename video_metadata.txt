**Real Map Implementation of Bidirectional A***

Witness the speed king of pathfinding in action! Our visualization showcases the **Bidirectional A* algorithm**, a remarkable optimization that drastically reduces search space by initiating two simultaneous searches. One search begins from the start node (e.g., one end of Market Street), expanding outwards, while the other starts from the goal node (the other end), expanding inwards. These two search frontiers intelligently expand by prioritizing nodes with the lowest estimated total cost (f-score), calculated as the sum of the actual cost from the start (g-score) and the heuristic estimate to the goal (h-score). This means that at each step, both searches consider neighbors that offer the most promising path towards the *other* search's current frontier, effectively narrowing down the search space much faster than a unidirectional A*.

Internally, Bidirectional A* maintains two separate data structures: typically, two **priority queues** (one for each search direction) to efficiently retrieve nodes with the lowest f-score. Each queue stores nodes, ordered by their f-score, along with their respective g-scores and parent pointers to reconstruct the path. As nodes are expanded, they are marked as visited for their respective search direction. When the search frontiers meet (i.e., a node explored by one search is encountered by the other), the algorithm has found the shortest path.

This visualization brings **real-world Graph Theory** to life using San Francisco's iconic Market Street. We've transformed this urban landscape into a graph where **nodes represent intersections** and **edges represent the street segments connecting them**. The vibrant red and green lines you see are the **"visited edges"** from our bidirectional searches, illustrating the intelligent exploration of this complex network. The bright **yellow line** is the computed shortest path, a testament to the algorithm's effectiveness. It's crucial to note the significant complexity difference between searching on explicit grids and real roads. Unlike uniform grids where neighbors are easily defined, real road networks have varying edge weights (travel times, distance, one-way restrictions) and a more complex connectivity, making algorithms like Bidirectional A* indispensable for efficient navigation.
