**Real Map Implementation of Bidirectional A\* 🗺️✨**

Ever wondered how navigation apps find the fastest route through bustling city streets? Meet Bidirectional A\*! This isn't just any search algorithm; it's the "speed king" for pathfinding on maps. Unlike traditional A\* that starts from a single point and expands outwards, Bidirectional A\* simultaneously runs *two* A\* searches: one forwards from the start node, and another backwards from the goal node. Each search maintains its own open and closed lists (typically implemented using priority queues for efficient neighbor selection based on the f-score: f(n) = g(n) + h(n), where g(n) is the cost from the start and h(n) is the heuristic estimate to the goal). The magic happens when these two search frontiers meet. The algorithm terminates when a node has been visited by *both* searches, effectively creating a handshake in the middle. This drastically reduces the explored search space compared to a single-direction search, especially in large or complex graphs, making it incredibly efficient for finding optimal paths.

Our visualization brings this powerful algorithm to life on the charming, intricate streets of Rome's Trastevere district! We've transformed this real-world location into a graph where intersections act as **nodes** and the connecting streets are **edges**. The red and green lines you see represent the edges being explored by our forward and backward searches, respectively – essentially, our "smart searches racing towards each other." The yellow line? That's the computed path, discovered by the algorithm when its two frontiers successfully met. Implementing pathfinding on real-world road networks introduces nuances not found in explicit grids. Road networks are often irregular, with varying edge costs (e.g., one-way streets, traffic conditions, speed limits), and can have significantly higher branching factors. Bidirectional A\* excels here because it tackles these complexities by intelligently pruning the search space from both ends, dramatically outperforming single-direction searches in both time and memory efficiency on these dense, interconnected maps.
