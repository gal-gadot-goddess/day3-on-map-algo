**Real Map Implementation of DFS 🗺️🧠**

Ever wondered how algorithms navigate complex pathways? This visualization showcases Depth-First Search (DFS) tackling the intricate streets of Mumbai's Colaba! 🇮🇳 At its core, DFS operates by exploring as far as possible along each branch before backtracking. When faced with multiple unvisited neighbors, DFS typically prioritizes one, diving deep into its connected path. This behavior is inherently recursive or managed using a LIFO (Last-In, First-Out) data structure – a **stack**. Unlike Breadth-First Search (BFS) which uses a queue to explore level by level, DFS's stack-based approach means it's all about depth. This "plunge deep" strategy can sometimes lead it down suboptimal paths, but its simplicity and ability to find *any* path quickly are key advantages.

Transforming a vibrant city like Mumbai into a graph for algorithmic analysis is fascinating! Here, each **intersection** acts as a **node**, and the **streets** connecting them are the **edges**. In this visualization, the **red and green lines** represent the edges being actively explored by our DFS algorithm, marking them as "visited" as it traverses the graph. The **shimmering yellow line** then highlights the final computed path found by DFS, demonstrating its journey from a starting point to a destination. It's important to note that real-world road networks are significantly more complex than typical grid-based graphs. Factors like one-way streets, varying street lengths, and traffic can drastically alter the actual traversal time and the effectiveness of algorithms like DFS compared to their performance on simpler, explicit grids. While DFS might be "fast" in terms of graph traversal logic, navigating a real city involves many more real-world constraints!
