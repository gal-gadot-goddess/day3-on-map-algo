**Real Map Implementation of Bidirectional A***

Ever wondered how navigation apps find the fastest route? Meet the **Bidirectional A* search algorithm**, the speed king of pathfinding! Unlike traditional A* that expands outwards from a single point, Bidirectional A* launches *two* searches simultaneously: one forward from the start node and one backward from the goal node. This dual-pronged attack significantly prunes the search space.

At its core, A* (and its bidirectional variant) uses a **priority queue** to manage nodes to visit, ordered by a cost function `f(n) = g(n) + h(n)`. `g(n)` is the known cost from the start to the current node `n`, and `h(n)` is a heuristic estimate of the cost from `n` to the goal. Bidirectional A* applies this to both searches independently. When the search frontiers (the sets of nodes visited by each search) *meet*, a path is found. The priority queue dynamically prioritizes neighbors based on their `f` score, ensuring exploration is directed towards the most promising areas, effectively "racing" towards each other.

Transforming a bustling cityscape like Paris's Marais district into a computable graph is a fascinating application of **Graph Theory**. Each intersection becomes a **node**, and each street segment connecting two intersections is an **edge**. The weight of an edge can represent travel time or distance. In this visualization, the **red and green lines** vividly illustrate the "visited edges" of our two independent A* searches. They show how the algorithm systematically explores the street network. The **yellow line** elegantly displays the final, computed shortest path. Unlike a perfect grid, real-world road networks have complex, non-uniform edge weights, traffic patterns, and one-way streets, making Bidirectional A*'s efficiency on such intricate graphs truly remarkable. The early meeting of the search frontiers dramatically reduces the number of nodes explored compared to a single-direction search.
