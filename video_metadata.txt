**Real Map Implementation of Greedy Best-First**

Ever wondered how navigation apps *try* to find the quickest route? This visualization showcases the **Greedy Best-First Search algorithm** in action on a real-world map of San Francisco's Market Street! 🗺️

At its core, Greedy Best-First prioritizes exploring the path that *appears* closest to the goal at each step, using a heuristic function. It doesn't look ahead to future possibilities like more exhaustive algorithms. We're using a **Priority Queue** here, where nodes (intersections) are ordered based on their estimated distance to the target intersection. When considering neighbors of the current intersection, the algorithm evaluates their heuristic value (e.g., straight-line distance to the goal) and adds the unvisited ones to the priority queue. It then *greedily* selects the node with the lowest heuristic value from the queue to explore next, effectively "running straight towards the target!" 🚀 This can be incredibly fast, but be warned: it's a "shortest path *heuristic*" and doesn't guarantee the *optimal* shortest path.

This isn't just an abstract grid! We've transformed a section of **San Francisco's Market Street** into a graph. Each **intersection** represents a **node**, and each **street segment** connecting two intersections is an **edge**. The red and green lines you see are the **visited edges**, illustrating the algorithm's exploration process as it traverses the graph. The final **yellow line** represents the path computed by the Greedy Best-First algorithm. Notice how the complexity of real-world road networks (one-way streets, varying traffic patterns, turns) adds layers of challenge not present in simple explicit grids. Our visualization models this by evaluating potential paths based on the graph's structure and the heuristic guiding the search.
