**Real Map Implementation of Bidirectional A***

Witness the "speed king" of pathfinding algorithms in action! Our visualization demonstrates Bidirectional A* on a real-world map of Mumbai's Colaba district. Instead of a single search radiating outwards, Bidirectional A* cleverly employs two search agents: one starting from the origin and another from the destination. These searches expand simultaneously, working towards a meeting point. Each agent uses a priority queue (often a min-heap) to store and explore nodes, prioritizing those with the lowest estimated total cost ($f(n) = g(n) + h(n)$), where $g(n)$ is the cost from the start node to node $n$, and $h(n)$ is the heuristic estimate of the cost from node $n$ to the goal. The magic happens when the two search frontiers intersect, effectively halving the search space and significantly boosting efficiency, especially in large and complex environments.

Transforming a bustling city like Mumbai into a traversable graph is a fascinating application of Graph Theory. Each intersection represents a **node**, and the streets connecting them are the **edges**. Edge weights are determined by real-world factors like street length and, critically for practical applications, estimated travel time (which can be dynamic!). In our visualization, the red and green lines vividly illustrate the **visited edges** of each search, showing the algorithm's exploration. The bright yellow line precisely pinpoints the **computed optimal path** found by Bidirectional A*. Unlike idealized grids where connectivity is uniform, real roads present a much more complex graph. The branching nature of streets, one-way systems, and varying traffic conditions create a nuanced search space where the intelligent pruning of Bidirectional A* truly shines, outperforming single-direction searches on such intricate terrains.
