## Real Map Implementation of DFS

Ever wondered how GPS navigates through the labyrinthine streets of a city like Berlin? Today, we're visualizing Depth-First Search (DFS) on a real-world map of Mitte! 🇩🇪

**How DFS Explores:** DFS is a graph traversal algorithm that plunges as deep as possible down each branch before backtracking. Imagine exploring a maze: you pick one path and follow it until you hit a dead end, then you retrace your steps and try another. In our visualization, DFS prioritizes exploring one adjacent "node" (intersection) at a time, going as far as it can. It typically uses a **stack** (either explicitly or implicitly through recursion) to keep track of the nodes and paths it needs to revisit. Unlike Breadth-First Search (BFS) which uses a queue to explore layer by layer, DFS's depth-first approach can sometimes lead it down long, unpromising paths, but it's often faster for certain problems and requires less memory than BFS in very deep graphs.

**Berlin's Streets as a Graph:** We've transformed a section of Berlin's Mitte district into a graph! Each intersection is a **node**, and the streets connecting them are **edges**. The red and green lines you see are "visited edges" – demonstrating how DFS systematically explores the network. The vibrant yellow line represents the computed path, highlighting a potential route found by our algorithm. Unlike a perfect grid, real-world road networks have varying edge weights (travel times/distances), one-way streets, and complex intersections. This adds a layer of complexity to graph algorithms; while DFS itself has a time complexity of O(V+E) (where V is vertices and E is edges), the *efficiency of finding an optimal path* on real roads can be significantly impacted by these real-world factors and the specific implementation choices.
