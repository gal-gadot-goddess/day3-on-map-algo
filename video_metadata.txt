**Real Map Implementation of Bidirectional A\***

Witness the speed king in action! Our visualization showcases the Bidirectional A\* algorithm navigating the bustling streets of Berlin (Mitte). This isn't just a theoretical concept; it's Graph Theory brought to life! At its core, Bidirectional A\* performs two simultaneous A\* searches: one forward from the start node and one backward from the goal node. Each search maintains its own set of data structures. Typically, a **priority queue** (often implemented with a min-heap) is crucial for both searches to efficiently store and retrieve nodes ordered by their estimated total cost (f-score = g-score + h-score). The **g-score** represents the cost from the start to the current node, and the **h-score** is the heuristic estimate from the current node to the goal. The algorithm intelligently expands the node with the lowest f-score in each direction, effectively narrowing the search space. When the two search frontiers meet, a complete path is found.

Our "Real Map Implementation" transforms the urban landscape of Berlin (Mitte) into a navigable graph. **Nodes** represent key intersections, and **edges** are the connecting streets. The red and green lines you see illustrate the **visited edges** – the exploration frontiers of our forward and backward searches, respectively. The vibrant yellow line? That's the dynamically computed optimal path connecting our start and end points. Unlike uniform grids where edge weights are often constant, real-world road networks introduce complexity. Edge costs can vary dramatically based on factors like one-way streets, traffic congestion (which we've simplified for visualization), turn restrictions, and road types. This makes Bidirectional A\* particularly advantageous, as the simultaneous searches quickly prune large portions of the graph, offering significant performance gains over a single-direction A\* on such complex, non-uniform graphs.
