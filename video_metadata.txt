**Real Map Implementation of Bidirectional BFS 🗺️✨**

Ever wondered how navigation apps find the fastest route? It's often not a simple one-way search! Our visualization showcases **Bidirectional Breadth-First Search (BFS)**, a powerful graph traversal algorithm that optimizes pathfinding. Unlike a standard BFS that expands outwards from a single source, Bidirectional BFS initiates two simultaneous searches: one from the **start node** and another from the **end node**. Both searches expand layer by layer, exploring neighbors using a **Queue** data structure for each search. The key advantage is efficiency: when the two expanding "frontiers" meet, a path is found. This dramatically reduces the search space, as the radius of exploration for each search is effectively halved compared to a single-direction BFS. This meeting point is determined by checking if a node visited by one search has already been visited by the other.

In this "Real Map Implementation," we've transformed the charming streets of **Rome's Trastevere** into a graph. Each intersection is a **node**, and the streets connecting them are **edges**. The red and green lines you see represent the expanding search fronts from our start and end points, visualizing the "visited edges" as the algorithm explores possible routes. The bright yellow line? That's the computed shortest path, found precisely when our two searches converged! While an explicit grid might have uniform edge weights, real-world road networks are more complex. Factors like traffic, one-way streets, and varying street lengths mean edge traversal isn't always constant, introducing nuances to the BFS's efficiency on these dynamic graphs.
