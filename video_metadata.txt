**Real Map Implementation of Greedy Best-First**

Ever wondered how algorithms navigate complex environments? This visualization showcases the Greedy Best-First search algorithm in action, mapping out Shibuya, Tokyo! 🏙️

Greedy Best-First prioritizes exploring nodes based solely on an evaluation function (often an estimate of the cost to the goal). In essence, it's a "greedy" approach – it always chooses the neighbor that appears to be *closest* to the target, without considering the cost incurred to reach that neighbor. This makes it incredibly fast, as it doesn't backtrack or explore suboptimal paths extensively. Technically, it typically uses a **priority queue** to manage the frontier of nodes to visit, ordered by their heuristic cost. This ensures the most promising node is always at the forefront of exploration. Think of it as always taking the road that *looks* like it goes the most directly towards your destination, irrespective of traffic or road conditions leading there.

Our "real map implementation" transforms the intricate streets of Shibuya into a digital graph. Each **intersection** serves as a **node**, and the **streets connecting them** are the **edges**. The visualization's **red and green lines** represent the explored edges of the graph, showing the algorithm's pathfinding journey. The **bright yellow line** depicts the final computed path – a testament to the algorithm's decision-making process. Unlike simple grid-based pathfinding, real-world road networks introduce complexities: varying edge weights (travel time, distance, one-way streets), dead ends, and the inherent lack of uniform connectivity. Greedy Best-First's speed advantage is amplified in such complex scenarios, though its heuristic-driven nature means it might not always find the *absolute shortest* path, but often a remarkably efficient one!
