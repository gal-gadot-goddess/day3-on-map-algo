**Real Map Implementation of Greedy Best-First 🗺️🚗💨**

Ever wondered how navigation apps find a path? Let's dive into a real-world application of graph theory with the Greedy Best-First search algorithm! Unlike other search methods, Greedy Best-First prioritizes exploration based on a heuristic function that estimates the cost from the current node to the target node. At each step, it expands the node that is "closest" to the goal. To achieve this, it typically utilizes a **priority queue** to store the fringe nodes, ordered by their heuristic value. This ensures that the algorithm always picks the most promising node to explore next, aiming to reach the target as quickly as possible by making locally optimal choices.

In this visualization, we've transformed the bustling streets of Downtown Dubai into a graph! Think of each intersection as a **node**, and the streets connecting them as **edges**. The red and green lines you see represent the **visited edges** as the Greedy Best-First algorithm explores the city's network, trying to find the target. The bright yellow line is the **computed path** it has determined. It's crucial to note that the complexity of navigating real-world road networks can be significantly different from explicit grid-based graphs. Factors like one-way streets, varying speed limits, and traffic congestion (which isn't explicitly modeled here) add layers of complexity that a simple grid doesn't capture, making heuristic choices even more impactful.
