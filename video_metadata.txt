## Real Map Implementation of Bidirectional BFS 🗺️

Ever wondered how navigation apps find the fastest route so quickly? This visualization demonstrates a powerful algorithm: **Bidirectional Breadth-First Search (BFS)**. Unlike a standard BFS that starts from a single source and expands outwards, Bidirectional BFS simultaneously initiates two searches: one from the starting point and another from the destination.

The core logic lies in its efficient expansion. Both searches operate on the same graph representation, prioritizing neighbors based on their distance from their respective starting points. In this implementation, we utilize **queues** to manage the nodes to be visited. As each search expands, it marks visited nodes and edges. When the two search frontiers meet, the algorithm has successfully found a path. This "meeting in the middle" significantly reduces the search space compared to a unidirectional BFS, effectively cutting the search time in half in many scenarios.

### From City Streets to Graph Nodes 🏙️➡️🔗

This visualization brings **Graph Theory** to life by transforming the streets of Berlin's Mitte district into a traversable graph. Each **intersection** in the city is represented as a **node**, and the **streets** connecting them are the **edges**. The red and green lines you see illustrate the "visited edges" of our two expanding BFS searches, dynamically exploring the street network. The shining yellow line is the computed shortest path, elegantly connecting our start and end points.

The beauty of this real-world application is its inherent complexity. Unlike a perfectly uniform grid, city road networks are irregular. This means edge weights (street lengths/travel times) can vary dramatically. While BFS is inherently designed for unweighted graphs, its bidirectional nature still offers significant performance gains. For scenarios with varying edge weights, Dijkstra's algorithm or A* search would be employed, but Bidirectional BFS provides a strong foundation and a visually intuitive approach to solving pathfinding problems in complex, real-world environments.
