**Real Map Implementation of Greedy Best-First**

Ever wondered how algorithms find the quickest way through a maze, or even a city? In this visualization, we're exploring the **Greedy Best-First search algorithm** in action, right on the charming streets of Paris's Marais district! 🇫🇷

At its core, Greedy Best-First is a highly efficient graph traversal algorithm that aims to reach the goal node as quickly as possible. Unlike algorithms that explore exhaustively, Greedy Best-First prioritizes its search based on a heuristic function that estimates the distance to the target. Imagine it as an agent always choosing the next step that *looks* closest to the destination, without necessarily considering the cost of that step or its long-term implications. This means it doesn't guarantee the *optimal* path (the absolute shortest), but it's often remarkably fast. In terms of data structures, it typically uses a **priority queue**. When exploring neighbors, the algorithm inserts them into the priority queue, ordered by their heuristic value (how "promising" they seem). It then repeatedly extracts the node with the lowest heuristic value from the queue and expands it, adding its unvisited neighbors back into the queue. This single-minded pursuit of immediate proximity is what defines its "greedy" nature.

This visualization brings Graph Theory to life on a real-world map! The intricate Parisian street network of Le Marais is represented as a graph: each **intersection** is a **node**, and each **street segment** connecting two intersections is an **edge**. The red and green lines you see trace the **visited edges** as the algorithm explores, expanding its search outward but always leaning towards the target. The bright yellow line showcases the **computed path** found by Greedy Best-First. Unlike a simple grid, real-world road networks introduce complexity: edge weights (street lengths) can vary drastically, and there are often one-way streets, traffic considerations (though not explicitly modeled here), and the sheer density of connections. This means the performance and path quality of Greedy Best-First can differ significantly from its behavior on a uniform grid, highlighting the challenges and nuances of applying algorithms to dynamic, real-world scenarios.
