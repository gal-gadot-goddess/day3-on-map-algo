## Real Map Implementation of Dijkstra

Ever wondered how your GPS finds the *exact* shortest path? At its core, it often relies on algorithms like Dijkstra's. This powerful technique systematically explores a network to identify the lowest-cost route from a starting point to all other reachable points. Dijkstra's algorithm operates on a principle of "greedy" expansion: it always selects the unvisited node with the smallest known distance from the source. This prioritization is typically achieved using a **priority queue**, which efficiently retrieves the minimum element. As nodes are processed, their neighbors' distances are updated. If a neighbor can be reached via a shorter path through the current node, its distance is revised, and it's re-prioritized in the queue. This process continues until the destination is reached or the priority queue is empty.

Transforming a real-world city like San Francisco, specifically Market Street, into a computational graph involves abstracting its physical layout. Each **intersection** becomes a **node** in our graph, and the **streets** connecting them become **edges**. The "weight" assigned to each edge can represent various factors, such as travel time, distance, or even traffic conditions. In this visualization, the red and green lines represent the "visited edges" as Dijkstra's algorithm explores the network, discovering potential paths. The vibrant yellow line meticulously traces the computed shortest path from our starting point to our destination. Unlike a simple grid where edge traversal is uniform, real-world road networks present complexities. Edge weights can vary drastically, and the graph is inherently sparse and irregular, requiring more sophisticated handling than a uniform grid.
