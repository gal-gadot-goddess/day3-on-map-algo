**Real Map Implementation of Bidirectional A***

🚀 Witness the speed king of pathfinding in action! This visualization showcases the Bidirectional A* algorithm, a remarkably efficient approach for finding the shortest path. Unlike traditional A*, Bidirectional A* initiates two simultaneous searches: one forward from the start node and one backward from the goal node. Each search operates independently, exploring its neighborhood based on a heuristic function (estimating the distance to the *other* search's starting point) and the actual cost to reach the current node.

Technically, both searches utilize a **priority queue** (often implemented with a min-heap) to store and retrieve nodes with the lowest f-score (g-score + h-score). The g-score represents the actual cost from the search's origin to the current node, while the h-score is the estimated cost to the *other* search's origin. Neighbors are prioritized based on this combined f-score, ensuring the most promising paths are explored first. The searches terminate when they meet, meaning a node has been visited by both forward and backward traversals. This convergence is key to its efficiency, as it drastically reduces the search space compared to a single-direction A* search.

🌍 This is not just an abstract grid; we're bringing graph theory to life in the iconic Eixample district of Barcelona! To achieve this "Real Map Implementation," we treat the city's intricate street network as a graph. Each **intersection** becomes a **node**, and the **streets connecting them** are the **edges**. The weights of these edges can represent real-world factors like street length, one-way restrictions, or even estimated travel times. In this visualization, the red and green lines you see are the "visited edges" – the exploration trails of our forward and backward searches respectively. The bright yellow line is the computed shortest path, discovered when these two intelligent searches finally converge. The complexity of pathfinding on real-world road networks is often far less predictable than on explicit grids due to the organic nature of urban planning, but Bidirectional A*'s ability to prune the search space makes it exceptionally well-suited for such complex, real-world graph problems.
