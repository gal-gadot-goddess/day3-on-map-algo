**Real Map Implementation of Bidirectional A***

Witness the elegance of graph theory in action! This visualization showcases the **Bidirectional A*** algorithm, a true speed king for pathfinding, especially on real-world maps. At its core, Bidirectional A* isn't just one search, but two intelligent searches concurrently exploring the graph – one from the start node and the other from the goal node. These two searches meet in the middle, drastically reducing the search space compared to a traditional unidirectional A*.

The magic happens through a **priority queue** (or often two, one for each search direction) which meticulously manages nodes to visit. Each node is evaluated based on its **f-score**, the sum of its **g-score** (the cost from the start to the current node) and its **h-score** (the estimated cost from the current node to the goal, using a heuristic like Euclidean distance). The algorithm prioritizes expanding nodes with the lowest f-score, ensuring it efficiently explores the most promising paths. When the two search frontiers "touch" (i.e., a node explored by one search is found in the open set of the other), the optimal path is found.

Now, let's talk about bringing London's bustling streets to life as a graph. In this **Real Map Implementation** of Soho, **nodes** represent intersections, and **edges** represent the streets connecting them. The red and green lines you see are the **visited edges** of our dual searches, dynamically expanding the graph's reach. The brilliant yellow line? That's the meticulously computed shortest path from your starting point to your destination, a testament to the algorithm's efficiency. Unlike rigid grids, real-world road networks are often sparse and can have complex edge weights (think one-way streets, traffic patterns, and varying road types). Bidirectional A*'s ability to prune search space is crucial here, making it significantly more efficient than its unidirectional counterpart on such intricate, non-uniform graphs.
