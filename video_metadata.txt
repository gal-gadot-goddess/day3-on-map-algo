## Real Map Implementation of Dijkstra 🇫🇷✨

Ever wondered how your GPS finds the fastest route through a maze of city streets? It's often powered by **Dijkstra's Algorithm**, a cornerstone of graph theory that finds the shortest path between nodes in a weighted graph. At its core, Dijkstra's operates by maintaining a set of visited nodes and a priority queue of unvisited nodes. It iteratively selects the unvisited node with the smallest known distance from the source, marks it as visited, and then updates the distances of its neighbors. This greedy approach ensures that once a node is visited, its shortest path from the source has been definitively found. The algorithm leverages a **priority queue** (often implemented with a min-heap) to efficiently retrieve the node with the minimum distance at each step, making it a highly effective method for finding optimal paths in scenarios where edge weights are non-negative.

This visualization brings Dijkstra's Algorithm to life on the charming streets of Paris's Marais district. To create this "real map implementation," we've transformed the city into a graph: each intersection serves as a **node**, and the streets connecting them are the **edges**. The "weight" of each edge isn't just distance, but could incorporate factors like typical traffic, speed limits, or even one-way restrictions. The pulsating **red and green lines** you see represent the algorithm actively "exploring" the graph, marking edges and nodes as visited while it calculates potential paths. The final **vibrant yellow line** showcases the exact shortest path determined by Dijkstra's, weighing every possible route. It's important to note that applying graph algorithms to real-world road networks is more complex than on simple grids. Unlike theoretical grids where every node has a fixed number of neighbors, real roads are dynamic and irregular, leading to significantly larger and more complex graph structures, requiring efficient implementations.
