**Real Map Implementation of Bidirectional Dijkstra 🗺️✨**

Ever wondered how the fastest route is truly found? This visualization demonstrates the power of **Bidirectional Dijkstra's Algorithm**, a true optimization marvel in pathfinding! Unlike its unidirectional counterpart, Bidirectional Dijkstra intelligently launches two simultaneous searches from both the start and end points. Each search maintains its own priority queue (typically a min-heap) to explore neighbors based on their cumulative edge weights. The algorithm prioritizes visiting nodes that are "closer" to their respective search origin, effectively reducing the search space. When the two search frontiers meet, the algorithm has guaranteed the shortest path. We're using priority queues here to efficiently extract the node with the smallest distance, ensuring we always expand from the most promising direction.

Witnessing this in the heart of Rome's Trastevere district brings Graph Theory to life! 🇮🇹 We've transformed the intricate network of streets and intersections into a graph: each intersection is a **node**, and each street segment connecting them is a weighted **edge**. The red and green lines you see represent the expanding search frontiers from our start and end points respectively, visualizing the "visited edges" as the algorithm explores possible paths. The striking yellow line is the computed **shortest path**, a testament to the algorithm's efficiency. It's fascinating to observe how the complexity of pathfinding on real-world road networks, with their inherent constraints and irregular structures, differs from simpler grid-based scenarios. Bidirectional Dijkstra excels by significantly pruning the search space in such complex, irregular graphs.
