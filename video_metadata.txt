**Real Map Implementation of Dijkstra**

Witness Dijkstra's Algorithm in action, navigating the intricate streets of London's Soho! At its core, Dijkstra is a greedy algorithm designed to find the shortest path between a starting node and all other nodes in a graph with non-negative edge weights. It operates by maintaining a set of visited nodes and a priority queue of unvisited nodes. The priority queue stores nodes ordered by their tentative distance from the source. In each step, Dijkstra extracts the unvisited node with the smallest tentative distance from the priority queue. For each neighbor of this extracted node, it calculates a new tentative distance by adding the edge weight to the extracted node's distance. If this new distance is shorter than the neighbor's current tentative distance, it updates the neighbor's distance and its predecessor (to reconstruct the path later), then re-inserts or updates the neighbor in the priority queue. This iterative process guarantees that we always explore the "closest" unvisited node first, eventually reaching the global optimum.

Our "Real Map Implementation" transforms the complex urban landscape of Soho into a traversable graph. Each intersection in Soho becomes a node, and the streets connecting them are the edges. The "weight" of each edge is determined by real-world factors like travel time, distance, or even traffic conditions (though for simplicity, we often use distance as the primary weight in visualizations). The vibrant red and green lines you see illustrate the algorithm's exploration process – the red lines represent edges being considered or processed, and the green ones denote edges that have been finalized as part of a shortest path to some node. The computed shortest path itself is highlighted in striking yellow. Unlike the predictable grids often used in theoretical examples, real-world road networks are far more complex. They feature varying edge weights (different street lengths, one-way systems, varying speed limits), dead ends, and complex intersections, making Dijkstra's robust approach essential for accurate navigation.
