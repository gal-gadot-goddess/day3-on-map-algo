**Real Map Implementation of DFS** 🗺️🚶‍♀️

Ever wondered how algorithms navigate complex networks? Today, we're diving deep into Depth-First Search (DFS) using a real-world visualization of Tokyo's vibrant Shibuya!

DFS operates by exploring as far as possible along each branch before backtracking. When it encounters a node (an intersection, in our case), it selects *one* unvisited neighbor and moves to it. This process continues recursively. The key data structure driving DFS is a **stack** (either explicitly managed or implicitly via function call stack in recursive implementations). Unlike Breadth-First Search (BFS) which uses a queue to explore layer by layer, DFS prioritizes depth, making it excellent for finding *any* path quickly, but potentially getting "lost" down long, unproductive branches.

In our Shibuya visualization, we've transformed the bustling city into a graph! Each intersection is a **node**, and the connecting streets are the **edges**. The **red and green lines** you see represent the **visited edges** as DFS explores the network, meticulously marking its traversal. The **vibrant yellow line** illustrates the **computed path** it ultimately found. On actual road networks, the complexity can differ significantly from a simple grid. Factors like one-way streets, varying street lengths, and the sheer scale of interconnectedness mean DFS's efficiency can be influenced by the graph's structure, but its fundamental principle of deep exploration remains.
