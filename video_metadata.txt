**Real Map Implementation of Greedy Best-First**

Ever wondered how navigation apps find a quick route? Our latest visualization dives into the **Greedy Best-First Search algorithm**, a pathfinding technique that prioritizes speed. Unlike algorithms that exhaustively explore all possibilities, Greedy Best-First makes a choice at each step based on a heuristic that estimates how close a neighbor is to the goal. It essentially "runs straight towards the target!" Technologically, it typically utilizes a **priority queue** to manage its "frontier" of nodes to visit. Each element in the priority queue is ordered by its heuristic value (e.g., straight-line distance to the target). When expanding a node, it adds all unvisited neighbors to the priority queue, and then always picks the neighbor with the *lowest* heuristic value to explore next. This greedy approach makes it computationally efficient but can sometimes lead it down a suboptimal path if it gets "stuck" in a locally good but globally poor direction.

This visualization brings the Greedy Best-First algorithm to life on a real-world map of Barcelona's Eixample district! We've transformed the city's intricate street network into a **graph data structure**. Here, **nodes represent intersections**, and **edges represent the streets connecting them**. The red and green lines you see are "visited edges" – showcasing the algorithm's exploration of the graph. The bright yellow line highlights the path computed by Greedy Best-First from the start to the target. Notice how it often takes direct, sweeping turns. Implementing this on complex, real-world road networks presents unique challenges compared to simple grids. Factors like one-way streets, varying street lengths, traffic patterns (though not explicitly modeled here), and the sheer density of connections mean the graph can become significantly more complex and dynamic, impacting both computational cost and the potential for suboptimal routes.
