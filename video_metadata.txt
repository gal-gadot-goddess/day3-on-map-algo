**Real Map Implementation of Greedy Best-First**

Ever wondered how algorithms navigate complex spaces? 🗺️ This visualization showcases the **Greedy Best-First Search** algorithm in action, mapping out a path through the vibrant streets of SoHo, New York City!

At its core, Greedy Best-First operates by prioritizing the neighbor node that appears to be closest to the target. It doesn't look ahead to evaluate the *total* cost of a path, but rather makes the locally optimal choice at each step. To achieve this, it typically employs a **priority queue**. This data structure is crucial because it allows us to efficiently retrieve the node with the lowest heuristic value (our estimated distance to the goal) at any given moment. When exploring a node, we add its unvisited neighbors to the priority queue, each with an associated heuristic cost. The algorithm then repeatedly extracts the highest-priority node (the one closest to the goal) from the queue and expands it, until the target is reached. This "greedy" approach makes it incredibly fast, as it rapidly moves towards the goal without extensive deliberation.

Our "Real Map Implementation" transforms a section of New York City's SoHo district into a navigable graph. Here, each **intersection** represents a **node**, and the **streets connecting them** form the **edges**. The red and green lines you see illustrate the edges being "visited" as the algorithm explores possibilities. The bright yellow line traces the computed path determined by Greedy Best-First. Notice how it seems to make a direct beeline towards our destination! This contrasts with simpler grid-based graphs where edge costs are uniform. In real-world road networks, edge traversal involves factors like one-way streets, traffic conditions, and varying distances, making the heuristic estimation more nuanced and potentially leading to suboptimal solutions if the heuristic isn't perfectly informed.
