**Real Map Implementation of DFS**

Ever wondered how algorithms navigate complex, interconnected spaces? Our latest visualization brings Depth-First Search (DFS) to life on the bustling streets of London's Soho! 🇬🇧 Let's dive into the technical details. DFS operates on the principle of exploring as far as possible along each branch before backtracking. When presented with multiple unvisited neighbors from a given node (an intersection, in our map analogy), DFS arbitrarily chooses one to proceed down, pushing it onto its internal *stack*. It continues this deep dive until it hits a dead end or an already visited node. Only then does it pop the last node from its stack and backtrack, exploring alternative paths. This inherent stack-based behavior makes it an extremely efficient choice for finding a path, though without careful management, it can indeed get "lost" in a vast labyrinth if not properly constrained.

This visualization transforms the intricate network of London's Soho into a tangible graph. Each intersection becomes a 'node', and the streets connecting them are the 'edges'. The red and green lines you see aren't just random colors; they represent the traversal process. The red lines meticulously trace the 'visited edges' as DFS explores the graph, meticulously marking its journey. The vibrant yellow line? That's the computed path, the triumphant outcome of DFS successfully navigating the urban maze. Unlike a clean, explicit grid where neighbor exploration is predictable, real-world road networks present unique challenges. The complexity lies not just in the sheer number of nodes and edges, but also in factors like one-way streets, varying street lengths, and the potential for cycles, which DFS handles by maintaining its 'visited' set. The speed of DFS on such graphs is remarkable, but understanding its traversal logic is key to appreciating its power.
