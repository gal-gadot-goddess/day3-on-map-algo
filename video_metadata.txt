**Real Map Implementation of DFS** 🗺️💡

Ever wondered how algorithms navigate complex spaces? Today, we're diving deep into **Depth-First Search (DFS)**, a powerful graph traversal algorithm, brought to life on the streets of London's Soho!

DFS operates by exploring as far as possible along each branch before backtracking. When it encounters a node, it systematically visits one of its unvisited neighbors. This "going deep" strategy is inherently recursive or can be implemented iteratively using a **stack**. Think of it like exploring a maze: you go down one path until you hit a dead end, then you retrace your steps to the last junction and try another path. Unlike algorithms that prioritize shortest paths (like Dijkstra's, which would use a priority queue), DFS simply picks an available neighbor, making it very fast in finding *a* path, though not necessarily the shortest. Its primary data structure for tracking nodes to visit is a **stack**, ensuring the most recently discovered path is explored first.

This visualization takes the abstract concept of a graph and grounds it in the vibrant reality of London's Soho. We've transformed the city's intricate network into a graph where **intersections act as nodes** and the **streets connecting them are the edges**. The **red and green lines** you see trace the algorithm's journey, marking **"visited edges"** as it explores the network. The **vibrant yellow line** represents the **computed path** found by DFS from a starting point to a destination. It's crucial to note that real-world road networks often present complexities absent in simple grids. Factors like one-way streets, traffic congestion, and differing street widths (which might implicitly affect travel time but not the raw graph structure) introduce nuances that explicit grid-based DFS implementations don't always capture, highlighting the power and limitations of algorithmic abstraction on complex real-world data.
