## Real Map Implementation of Greedy Best-First 🗺️✨

Ever wondered how pathfinding algorithms navigate the labyrinthine streets of a city like Rome? In this visualization, we're bringing the **Greedy Best-First Search** algorithm to life on a real map of the charming Trastevere district! 🇮🇹

### Unpacking the Greedy Best-First Logic 🧠
Greedy Best-First is a graph traversal algorithm that operates with a singular focus: **getting to the goal as quickly as possible, based on a heuristic evaluation.** Unlike algorithms like Dijkstra's that consider the *actual cost* to reach a node, Greedy Best-First prioritizes neighbors based on their estimated distance to the target. At each step, it examines all unvisited neighbors of the current node and selects the one that *appears* to be closest to the destination. This heuristic function is key, and in our visualization, it's simply the Euclidean distance. To manage its exploration, Greedy Best-First typically utilizes a **priority queue**. This data structure efficiently allows us to insert new nodes with their associated heuristic costs and then extract the node with the *minimum* heuristic cost at each iteration. This "greedy" approach means it doesn't backtrack or consider alternative paths that might be longer initially but ultimately lead to a better solution.

### Trastevere's Streets as a Graph: A Real-World Application 🏘️
This isn't your average grid! We've transformed the intricate network of streets in Rome's Trastevere into a **graph**. Each **intersection** is a **node** (or vertex), and each **street segment connecting two intersections** is an **edge**. The **red and green lines** you see are the **visited edges**, representing the algorithm's exploration as it traverses the graph. The **yellow line** signifies the computed path from our starting point to the destination. Implementing pathfinding on real-world road networks presents unique complexities compared to explicit grids. Factors like one-way streets, varying speed limits (edge weights), and the irregular, non-uniform nature of urban layouts mean that even simple algorithms can lead to fascinating and sometimes suboptimal outcomes. Greedy Best-First, while incredibly fast due to its direct approach, might miss a slightly longer but more efficient route because it's always chasing the most immediate perceived shortcut, potentially leading it down a winding alley when a straighter, albeit initially more distant, path could have been better.
